Law & Policy

NAGPRA

Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act

Federal Law1990

The Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act (NAGPRA) is a federal law enacted on November 16, 1990. It requires federal agencies and institutions that receive federal funding to return Native American cultural items and human remains to lineal descendants, and to culturally affiliated Indian tribes and Native Hawaiian organizations.

The law covers five categories of cultural items: human remains, associated funerary objects, unassociated funerary objects, sacred objects, and objects of cultural patrimony. It also establishes criminal penalties for illegal trafficking in Native American human remains and cultural items, and protects Native American graves on federal and tribal lands.

NAGPRA created a process â€” not a single event. Compliance has been uneven across institutions, and repatriation continues decades after the law's passage. The 2023 revised regulations significantly strengthened tribal consultation requirements and placed a greater burden of proof on holding institutions.

Cultural Affiliation

Legal TermNAGPRA

Under NAGPRA, cultural affiliation is defined as "a relationship of shared group identity which can be reasonably traced historically or prehistorically between a present-day Indian tribe or Native Hawaiian organization and an identifiable earlier group."

Establishing affiliation is the central challenge of the repatriation process. It draws on multiple lines of evidence: archaeological, geographical, kinship, biological, linguistic, folkloric, oral tradition, historical, and other relevant data. No single type of evidence is required or sufficient. Tribal oral histories and traditional knowledge are legally recognized as valid evidence alongside scientific data.

Importantly, the standard is "reasonable" connection, not certainty â€” acknowledging that colonial disruption, incomplete records, and institutional erasure can make definitive proof impossible.

25 U.S.C. Â§ 3001(2); 43 C.F.R. Â§ 10.14

Repatriation

ProcessNAGPRA

Repatriation refers to the transfer of cultural items and human remains from holding institutions back to culturally affiliated tribal nations or lineal descendants. Under NAGPRA, this process is both a legal obligation and a political act â€” one embedded in broader questions of Indigenous sovereignty, historical justice, and the right of communities to care for their own ancestors.

The physical transfer of objects is one part of a longer relationship. Many tribal nations describe repatriation as a spiritual and communal process of healing, and have established specific protocols for receiving and reinterring ancestral remains. For some communities, the process of consultation and recognition is as significant as the transfer itself.

Repatriation is not complete: tens of thousands of Native American individuals remain in institutional collections across the country, and the 2023 regulatory revisions renewed efforts to accelerate return.

National Park Service, National NAGPRA Program

Federal Register

Government Publication

The Federal Register is the official daily journal of the United States federal government, published by the National Archives. Under NAGPRA, institutions are required to publish two types of notices in the Federal Register to formalize the repatriation process.

A Notice of Inventory Completion announces that an institution has completed its inventory of human remains and associated funerary objects and has determined cultural affiliation with one or more tribes. A Notice of Intent to Repatriate announces plans to return unassociated funerary objects, sacred objects, or objects of cultural patrimony. Both notices initiate a 30-day public comment period before transfer may proceed.

These notices are the primary data source for this mapping project.

43 C.F.R. Â§ 10.9â€“10.10

Inventory

NAGPRA Process

Under NAGPRA, an inventory is a written itemized list of human remains and associated funerary objects held by a federal agency or institution. The law required all covered institutions to complete their initial inventories by November 16, 1995, and to consult with potentially affiliated tribes during the process.

Inventories must include the geographic and cultural affiliation of each item where known, and must be shared with all potentially affiliated tribes. Inventories are distinct from summaries, which apply to other categories of cultural items.

25 U.S.C. Â§ 3003; 43 C.F.R. Â§ 10.9

Summary

NAGPRA Process

A summary is a written description â€” not an item-by-item list â€” of unassociated funerary objects, sacred objects, and objects of cultural patrimony held by a covered institution. The law required summaries to be completed by November 16, 1993, two years before the inventory deadline.

The distinction between inventory and summary reflects the different legal frameworks governing human remains versus cultural objects. Summaries are intended to provide tribal nations with enough information to determine whether to request repatriation of specific items.

25 U.S.C. Â§ 3004; 43 C.F.R. Â§ 10.8

Lineal Descendant

Legal Category

A lineal descendant is an individual who can trace direct biological descent from a specific Native American individual whose remains or cultural items are held by an institution. Lineal descendants have the highest priority claim under NAGPRA â€” above tribal affiliation claims.

In practice, lineal descent is often difficult to establish given the time depth of many collections and the effects of colonial displacement on family and community records.

25 U.S.C. Â§ 3001(10); 43 C.F.R. Â§ 10.2(b)(1)

Tribal Consultation

ProcessSovereignty

Tribal consultation is the process by which federal agencies and covered institutions engage with tribal governments on a government-to-government basis to discuss NAGPRA inventories, cultural affiliation determinations, and repatriation. It is a legal requirement, not a courtesy.

The quality of consultation varies enormously across institutions. Critics have noted that many institutions treat consultation as a bureaucratic checkbox rather than a genuine partnership. The 2023 regulatory revisions strengthened consultation requirements, including mandating that institutions obtain free, prior, and informed consent before conducting any research on Native American human remains.

43 C.F.R. Â§ 10.11; Executive Order 13175

Culturally Unidentifiable Human Remains

Legal CategoryContested

"Culturally unidentifiable" (CUI) is the designation applied to Native American human remains for which no cultural affiliation has been determined. For two decades after NAGPRA's passage, this category was used to hold tens of thousands of individuals in institutional collections without initiating repatriation.

The 2010 regulations addressed CUI remains for the first time, requiring institutions to offer them to tribes with ancestral ties to the geographic area from which they were excavated. The 2023 revisions went further, essentially ending the use of CUI as a reason to delay repatriation. Many tribal nations and scholars had long argued that "culturally unidentifiable" reflected institutional failure to consult adequately, not a genuine absence of cultural connection.

43 C.F.R. Â§ 10.11 (2010); 88 Fed. Reg. 92,716 (2023)

Cultural Items

Human Remains

NAGPRA Category

Under NAGPRA, human remains refers to the physical remains of the body of a person of Native American ancestry. This includes skeletal material, teeth, hair, mummified tissue, and other biological material. It does not include items that were once part of the body but have been severed and transformed into cultural objects (such as certain ceremonial items made from hair or bone).

Human remains are subject to inventory requirements and are the most legally protected category under NAGPRA. Their collection and retention by institutions has been a central issue of Indigenous advocacy for over a century.

25 U.S.C. Â§ 3001(9); 43 C.F.R. Â§ 10.2(d)(1)

Funerary Objects

NAGPRA Category

Funerary objects are items that, as part of a death rite or ceremony, were intentionally placed with individual human remains either at the time of death or later. NAGPRA distinguishes between associated funerary objects â€” those held with the human remains they were placed alongside â€” and unassociated funerary objects â€” those held separately from the remains, or where the remains cannot be located.

Associated funerary objects are subject to inventory requirements alongside human remains. Unassociated funerary objects are addressed in summaries and require tribal notification but follow slightly different repatriation procedures.

25 U.S.C. Â§ 3001(3)(A)â€“(B)

Sacred Objects

NAGPRA Category

Sacred objects are defined under NAGPRA as specific ceremonial objects that are needed by traditional Native American religious leaders for the practice of traditional Native American religions by their present-day adherents. The emphasis on present-day religious use distinguishes sacred objects from other categories â€” an object must have ongoing ceremonial significance, not merely historical or archaeological value.

This definition has been contested, as it places the burden on tribal communities to demonstrate active religious practice and can require disclosure of sensitive ceremonial knowledge in order to assert a claim.

25 U.S.C. Â§ 3001(3)(C)

Cultural Patrimony

NAGPRA Category

Objects of cultural patrimony are items that have ongoing historical, traditional, or cultural importance central to a Native American group or culture itself, and that were not owned by any individual tribal member but were communally owned by the entire group. Because of this collective ownership, such items could not have been legally sold or given away by any individual.

This category is often the most legally complex to establish, requiring demonstration of communal ownership and significance. Examples may include clan regalia, ceremonial poles, and other items central to the political and cultural identity of a tribe or community.

25 U.S.C. Â§ 3001(3)(D)

Tribal & Political Status

Tribal Sovereignty

PoliticalLegal

Tribal sovereignty refers to the inherent authority of Indigenous nations to govern themselves, their members, and their territories. It is not a right granted by the federal government but an original, pre-constitutional authority that has been partially â€” and unevenly â€” recognized and constrained by U.S. law.

In the context of NAGPRA, tribal sovereignty is foundational: the law treats tribal nations as governments, not ethnic groups or interest organizations, and requires consultation on a government-to-government basis. Repatriation is therefore not merely a cultural or humanitarian gesture but an exercise of tribal sovereignty over ancestral remains and cultural heritage.

Worcester v. Georgia, 31 U.S. 515 (1832); United States v. Lara, 541 U.S. 193 (2004)

Federally Recognized Tribe

Political Status

A federally recognized tribe is a Native American tribal entity that the U.S. government has formally acknowledged to have a government-to-government relationship with the United States. Recognition confers eligibility for federal programs and services, protections under federal Indian law, and standing to make NAGPRA claims.

As of 2024, there are 574 federally recognized tribes in the contiguous United States and Alaska. Recognition does not determine the legitimacy or cultural continuity of a tribal nation â€” many tribes with documented histories remain unrecognized due to historical policies and bureaucratic barriers.

Bureau of Indian Affairs, Federal Register, Vol. 89 (2024)

State Recognized Tribe

Political Status

A state recognized tribe is a tribal nation acknowledged by an individual U.S. state but not by the federal government. State recognition does not grant the same legal rights or protections as federal recognition, and state-recognized tribes do not have standing to make NAGPRA claims unless they can demonstrate affiliation with a federally recognized tribe.

Many state-recognized tribes are pursuing federal recognition through the Bureau of Indian Affairs acknowledgment process, a lengthy and often prohibitive administrative procedure.

Native Hawaiian Organization (NHO)

Political StatusNAGPRA

Under NAGPRA, a Native Hawaiian Organization is any organization that serves and represents the interests of Native Hawaiians, has as a primary and stated purpose the provision of services to Native Hawaiians, and has expertise in Native Hawaiian affairs. NHOs have the same standing as federally recognized tribes to make NAGPRA claims on behalf of Native Hawaiian ancestors and cultural items.

Native Hawaiians occupy a distinct legal status: they are not a federally recognized tribe under the Indian Self-Determination Act, but NAGPRA specifically extended its protections to include them.

25 U.S.C. Â§ 3001(11)

Scholarly & Methodological

Bioarchaeology

DisciplineAnthropology

Bioarchaeology is the archaeological study of human skeletal remains recovered from archaeological contexts. It integrates methods from biological anthropology, osteology, and archaeology to reconstruct past lifeways, health, diet, migration, and social organization.

The discipline has a contested relationship with Indigenous communities: much of its foundational data was collected through the excavation of Native American burials â€” often without consent, and often in violation of Indigenous protocols. The growth of Indigenous critiques of bioarchaeology, and calls to "Indigenize" its methods and ethics, is a central concern of this project's scholarly framework.

Buikstra, J.E. & Lane Beck, L.A. (Eds.) (2006). Bioarchaeology: The Contextual Analysis of Human Remains.

Indigenizing

MethodologyTheory

Indigenizing refers to the process of recentering Indigenous knowledge systems, values, methods, and voices within academic disciplines that have historically marginalized or excluded them. In anthropology and bioarchaeology specifically, it involves critically examining whose questions are being asked, whose methods are privileged, and who benefits from research on Indigenous peoples and their ancestors.

Indigenizing is distinct from "including" Indigenous perspectives as supplementary â€” it involves a fundamental reorganization of research priorities, methodologies, and accountability structures, often requiring non-Indigenous scholars to cede interpretive authority to Indigenous communities.

Smith, L.T. (1999). Decolonizing Methodologies: Research and Indigenous Peoples. Zed Books.

Traditional Knowledge

EpistemologyLegal

Traditional knowledge (TK) refers to the knowledge, innovations, and practices of Indigenous and local communities accumulated over generations through direct contact with the environment. In the context of NAGPRA and repatriation, TK includes oral histories, ceremonial knowledge, and community memories that document ancestral connections to specific places, objects, and individuals.

NAGPRA explicitly recognizes oral tradition as a valid form of evidence for establishing cultural affiliation, a significant legal acknowledgment that Western scientific evidence is not the only legitimate form of knowledge. Debates continue about whether TK should be treated as equivalent to, supplementary to, or independent from archaeological and biological evidence.

Indigenous Epistemology

TheoryPhilosophy

Epistemology is the branch of philosophy concerned with the nature, sources, and limits of knowledge â€” how we know what we know. Indigenous epistemologies are the distinct ways of knowing developed by specific Indigenous communities, often rooted in relationships with land, kinship, ceremony, and oral tradition rather than empiricist or positivist methodologies.

In anthropology and related fields, engaging with Indigenous epistemologies means recognizing that Western science is not the only legitimate framework for understanding the world â€” and that imposing it on Indigenous subjects and communities can be a form of epistemic violence.

Cajete, G. (2000). Native Science: Natural Laws of Interdependence. Clear Light Publishers.

Mapping & Data

Cultural Region

MappingAnthropology

A cultural region (or culture area) is a geographic area in which several Native American peoples share broadly similar cultural traits, subsistence patterns, and environmental adaptations. The concept was developed by early 20th-century anthropologists â€” particularly Clark Wissler and Alfred Kroeber â€” to organize museum collections and comparative research.

Cultural regions are a classification tool, not a statement about tribal identity or political boundaries. They have been criticized for flattening internal diversity, ignoring tribal sovereignty, and reflecting the priorities of the ethnographers who created them rather than the self-understanding of Indigenous communities. On this map, cultural regions provide geographic context for NAGPRA inventories but should not be read as defining or limiting the identities of the tribal nations within them.

Kroeber, A.L. (1939). Cultural and Natural Areas of Native North America. University of California Press.
